Austin History Center — A Typographical Intervention

Community Place-Making, UX Research

Introduction

Austin History Center is the self-described local history and archives division of the Austin Public Library. It is located next to Faulk Center Library at 810 Guadalupe Street in Austin, Texas. Open to the public since 1933, the library was the first to occupy the limestone building. The Austin-Travis County Collection began in 1955 as a single file and has been growing wildly since!

The site hosts collections to suit many needs, events and exhibitions. They not only provide the public with information about the local history and events in Austin, but aim to preserve these stories for future access. For more than seven decades now, the Austin History Center staff has supported activities, exhibits, and research. They are now open six days per week.

Direct Observations

This 8th & Guadalupe Street block is bustling with visitors of the library and the center, local pedestrians and traffic from cars, taxis and Capital Metro buses. Older and young adults, some with children, waiting for the bus or parking to visit the library surround the area. Some people seek immediate and friendly refuge inside the history center to use the bathrooms, or just shade and cool off. Wooldridge Square, a small knoll, covers the block north of the history center. Traffic flows one way southbound on Guadalupe, eastbound on 9th Street or westbound on 8th. There are two bus stops on Guadalupe Street; one in front of the history center and another in front of the public library. During most of the day, Metro Rapid runs every 15 minutes and there are a total of 14 Capital Metro routes that stop at that block.

Gloria Espitia, Austin History Center Neighborhood Liason:

So many people do not know about the center. They […] see the building, but they really don’t know much about it.

Secondary Sources

Visitors to the Austin History Center include those interested in the exhibitions, researchers and a small number of pedestrians that come to the park or are waiting on the bus. During busy exhibition openings, the Austin History Center reports over 700 visitors. On average, the exhibitions may see 200–400 people per day. The Reading Room at the end of the hall isn’t frequented as often as the exhibition and event rooms. That room sees 20–30 researchers on a busy day and only four on a slow day. Those interested in the actual archives include university students, from UT as well as other national and state colleges, tourists interested in the history of the city, and other serious investigators.

Tim Hamblin, Austin History Center Video Archivist:

I would love to encourage people to come down to the history center to check it out.

Objective

The ultimate goal is to attract the unknowing, pedestrians, newcomers and new Austinites to visit and learn more about the history center. The audience includes the general public that may not particularly be interested in history. After compiling data and research, it was determined that it would be best to utilize Capital Metro and other transportation services that travel around town and near the location.

Since the population of Austin, Texas has been continuously experiencing a boom, my goal is to help new Austinites learn the friendly vibe of the city and help them discover the history of their surroundings.

I not only want to introduce them to the Austin History Center, but also divert them to the location. The actual design solution shall exude the feeling of a casual invitation. New audiences should feel welcomed to enjoy and visit the center to explore all the resources and events.

As mentioned before, these invitations shall become mobile when displayed as posters in Metro buses, taxis, and pedicabs. As part of meeting the objective, the posters will fortunately be apparent and visible to many locals in popular neighborhoods, well-known streets, and near landmarks, including the history center and the library.
